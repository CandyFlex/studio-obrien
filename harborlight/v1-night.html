2:14 AMwhat happens after we pick up

How a rescue actually goes.

Not a mission statement. The literal steps, with the literal clock, for the terrier from Route 9.

  1. Minute 0

    The call.

    The 24-hour line rings to whichever volunteer drew the short straw this week. We ask where, we ask how bad, and we're driving before the second question is answered.

  2. Hour 1

    Intake.

    Warm crate, chip scan, photos, and Dr. Okafor's exam if it can't wait for morning. Every animal gets a name that night, even the ones who won't need it long. Especially those.

  3. Day 2

    A couch.

    No kennels, no runs, no concrete. Every Harborlight animal sleeps in a volunteer's home, starting the first night. Decompression takes as long as it takes.

  4. Week 2

    The workup.

    Vaccines, spay or neuter, microchip, dental if needed. Then the foster writes the bio, because the person who shares a couch with a dog is the only one qualified to describe it.

  5. One morning

    The door.

    Adoption day is always scheduled for morning. Partly logistics. Mostly because it feels correct that it happens in daylight.

5:12 AMthe honest ask

The bottleneck is not money. It is bedrooms.

Every animal we say yes to sleeps in a volunteer's home that same night. So the number of animals we can save is exactly the number of couches we can call at midnight. Right now that number is 61. It should be a hundred.

We cover everything. Food, vet bills, litter, crates, gas to appointments. Fostering costs you nothing but the couch and the goodbye.

The average stay is three weeks. Some are three days. The record is eleven months, and Doreen would tell you it was worth every one.

Foster fails are not failures. Around here we call it adopting with extra steps. It has happened 74 times. We keep a list. It is a happy list.

6:03 AMthe ledger's other side

What the night costs.

No gala, no swag, no development office. Your money buys the things on this list and nothing else. We publish the books every January.

$12 a month keeps the phone line and the porch light on. Literally. That is the power bill and the phone bill, divided by the people willing to share it.
