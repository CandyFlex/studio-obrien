Our Story

More Than Just
a Screen

In August 2018, Dave Walker and a friend converted a storefront on Main Street in Forest City, NC into something the town had never seen. Over 100 classic arcade cabinets and pinball machines, all powered up, all on freeplay. No tokens. No quarters. Just pay at the door and play everything.

He built it for people his age, the 30-to-50 crowd chasing the feeling of a childhood spent in arcades. But families showed up. Teenagers showed up. First dates. Birthday parties. Whole communities started treating it like a gathering place. What started as a nostalgia project became a genuine destination for anyone who wanted to put down their phone and play side by side.

"You have more feedback, and more fun when you're playing with somebody, and you're there physically. It's more than just a screen you're looking at."

Dave Walker, Founder

From an empty storefront to one of the most talked-about attractions in the NC Foothills. Here's how the Carolina Arcade Museum came together.

August 2018

The Doors Open

Dave Walker and a friend convert a vacant storefront on Main Street in downtown Forest City, NC into a living museum of arcade history. The initial collection fills the floor with classic cabinets and pinball tables, every single one set to freeplay. No tokens. No quarters. Just pay at the door and play everything.

2019

The Collection Grows

Word spreads fast. What started as a spot for nostalgic adults quickly becomes a family destination. The collection expands past 100 machines, with 80+ more rotating in from storage. Teenagers and kids discover the magic of physical, side-by-side gaming for the first time.

PBS North Carolina Weekend sends a crew to Forest City. The segment captures what makes this place different: it's not about screens in isolation. It's about standing next to someone, sharing the experience.

Chapter 01

How It Started

In August 2018, Dave Walker and a friend converted a vacant storefront on Main Street in downtown Forest City, NC into a living museum of arcade history. Over 100 classic arcade cabinets and pinball machines filled the floor, every single one set to freeplay. No tokens. No quarters. Just pay at the door and play everything. The idea was simple: recreate the social experience of 1980s arcade culture in a town that never had anything like it.

Chapter 02

The Why

Dave built the museum to bring back something he felt was disappearing: the experience of playing together in the same room. Not online. Not through a headset. Shoulder to shoulder, hands on the controls, reacting to the same screen at the same time. Approximately 105 games live on the floor today, with another 80+ in storage rotating in regularly. "You have more feedback, and more fun when you're playing with somebody, and you're there physically. It's more than just a screen you're looking at." Dave Walker, Founder

Chapter 03

What It Became

Dave originally built it for people his age, the 30-to-50 crowd chasing the feeling of a childhood spent in arcades. But families showed up. Teenagers showed up. First dates and birthday parties. Whole communities started treating it like a gathering place. What started as a nostalgia project became a destination for anyone who wants to put down their phone and play side by side.
