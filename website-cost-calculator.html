What is driving this estimate?

    Why website prices vary so widely

    A website can mean a modified template, a focused freelance build, a connected studio process, or a large agency engagement. This tool compares those routes using the same selected scope so you can see what you are actually paying for.

    Scope and content

    Page count matters, but page types, writing needs, content migration, and information architecture determine far more of the real workload.

    Functionality

    Booking, commerce, multilingual content, owner editing, and integrations add planning, configuration, testing, and support requirements.

    Creative depth

    Adapting a proven structure costs less than developing original art direction, writing, responsive behavior, and interaction patterns around one business.

    Sources and methodology: how the comparison is calculated
    1. Labor context uses the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics wage data for web developers and digital interface designers.
    2. Freelance rate context references publicly visible marketplace bands from Upwork. Marketplace rates vary greatly by experience, specialty, and geography.
    3. Agency context references Clutch's web design pricing guide, including its published hourly-rate bands and scope factors.
    4. The calculator estimates production effort from page types, selected functionality, and design depth, then applies broad provider-rate bands and overhead assumptions.
    5. Results are rounded planning ranges, not quotes. Content readiness, integrations, accessibility, timeline, decision-making, and support can materially change a real proposal.

    Frequently asked questions

    Is this a Studio O'Brien quote?

    No. It is a market-planning estimate showing what similar scope can cost through different provider types. Studio O'Brien recommends scope and pricing after learning what the business needs.

    Why are the ranges so broad?

    Provider experience, creative depth, content readiness, technical requirements, geography, process, and overhead all affect cost. A narrow instant number would create false precision.

    Is a template always the wrong choice?

    No. A template can be sensible for a simple need, a limited budget, and an owner willing to handle more of the work. Custom work becomes valuable when distinction, writing, functionality, and a connected customer experience matter.

    What costs are often forgotten?

    Copywriting, photography, content migration, accessibility, integrations, analytics, training, hosting, maintenance, and internal review time are commonly left out of early estimates.

    How should I compare proposals?

    Compare the actual scope, who is doing the work, what content and revisions are included, ownership terms, launch support, recurring costs, and what happens when requirements change.
